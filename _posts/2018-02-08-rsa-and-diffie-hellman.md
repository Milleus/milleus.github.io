### Rivest–Shamir–Adleman (RSA)
1. Clown A wants to communicate to Clown B.
2. Clown B generates RSA key pair; public and private key.
3. Clown B gives Clown A his public key.
4. Clown A encrypts his message with Clown B's public key.
5. Clown B receives encrypted message and decrypts it with his private key.

In summary, when giving shit out to others, it's always public key (that's why it's public duh!). Always keep private key. Similar concept is used in [Rebecca - Gaining authorized SSH access to another mac PC](/blog/2017/11/20/gaining-authorized-ssh-access-to-another-mac-pc){:target="_blank"}.

### Diffie Hellman (DH)
1. Clown A and Clown B wants to communicate.
2. Clown A and Clown B agrees to use identical boxes; boxes represent prime numbers *g* and *p*.
3. Clown A has a box and he locks it; lock represents secret number *a*.
4. Clown B has a box and he locks it; lock represents secret number *b*.
5. Clown A and Clown B exchange boxes.
6. Clown A adds his lock on the box with B's lock; (g<sup>b</sup> mod p)<sup>a</sup> mod p = g<sup>ba</sup> mod p
7. Clown B adds his lock on the box with A's lock; (g<sup>a</sup> mod p)<sup>b</sup> mod p = g<sup>ab</sup> mod p
8. Both clowns now have similar boxes cause math is magic!

In summary, an identical shared secret key is generated by both cause math too stronk. Safer because both parties do not know each other's secret number as it is not passed over.
